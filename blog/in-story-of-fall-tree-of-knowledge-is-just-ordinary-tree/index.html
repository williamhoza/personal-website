In the story of the Fall, the Tree of Knowledge is just an ordinary tree [draft]

Until recently, I took it for granted that God forbids eating the fruit because of the knowledge it confers. That's how St. Theophilus of Antioch interprets the story [1], so I still don't think it's a completely crazy interpretation.

However, according to other luminaries such as St. John Chrysostom and St. Augustine of Hippo, the correct interpretation is the other way around. The fruit confers knowledge only because it is forbidden!

When I learned this idea, it blew my mind. After rereading the story and studying the matter a bit, I totally buy it. The story makes way more sense to me now. Let me elaborate about how I understand the story now that St. John and St. Augustine (and others) have enlightened me.

Why God forbids eating the fruit

Everything God makes is very good (Genesis 1:31), including the Tree of Knowledge. St. Augustine says, they were forbidden the use of a tree, which, if it had not been for the prohibition, they might have used without suffering any evil result whatever [2, Book II, Chapter 35]. The tree's fruit is not poisonous or magical. In fact, other than the fact that its fruit is prohibited, I think the Tree of Knowledge is just a completely ordinary tree.

God forbids Adam and Eve from eating the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge because He wants to forbid something, not because the tree is bad or dangerous. St. John Chrysostom writes, The loving Lord, you see, instructing the human being in the beginning and from the very outset, and wanting to teach him that he has a creator and craftsman who produces all visible realities and shapes him as well, wished to reveal to him His own dominion through this slight command... He bids him stay away from the one tree, setting a severe penalty for transgression so that he may be aware he is under His dominion and along with everything else is a partaker of His generosity [3]. Similarly, St. Augustine writes, It was proper that man, placed in a state of dependence upon the Lord God, should be given some prohibition, so that obedience would be the virtue by which he would please his Lord [4, page 42]. The commandment to not eat the fruit is a kind of test. It can be compared to the test that Abraham faces when God tells him to sacrifice Isaac.

How Adam and Eve learn that they are naked

If the fruit is just ordinary fruit, then why do Adam and Eve realize they are naked? As I understand it, the answer is as follows.

  • First, when Adam and Eve are created, there is nothing wrong with being naked. Even if someone had pointed out to them that they were naked, they would not have felt any need to cover themselves.
  • Then, the act of sinning corrupts their relationships with their bodies. I see in my members another principle at war with the law of my mind (Romans 7:23). Consequently, it isn't good to go through life naked anymore.
  • Finally, the badness of nakedness causes Adam and Eve to notice that they are naked. Similarly, stepping out of a warm house into a snowstorm could open my eyes to the fact that I'm not wearing a jacket.

Thus, the act of sinning is what opens Adam and Eve's eyes, not anything in the fruit per se. Any sin would have done it.

What Adam and Eve learn about good and evil

After Adam and Eve confess to eating the forbidden fruit, God describes how they will be punished. Then God says, See! The man has become like one of us, knowing good and evil! (Genesis 3:22) God is confirming that one of the snake's statements has come true — but only in a terrible and ironic way. When Adam and Eve eat the forbidden fruit, they learn the painful consequences of sin. That's some "knowledge of good and evil" that they didn't have before. Thus, they do gain knowledge of good and evil, but only because they sin and are punished, not because the fruit has miraculous powers.

Notice that under this interpretation, the story is clever and maybe even a little funny. It reminds me of the story of King Croesus of Lydia. According to legend, the Oracle of Delphi told Croesus that if he attacked Persia, he would destroy a great empire. Croesus therefore attacked, expecting to destroy the Persian empire. Instead, he was defeated and his own empire was destroyed, ironically fulfilling the Oracle's prophesy.

Why the tree is called the Tree of Knowledge

St. Thomas Aquinas writes that the tree is called the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil because, after eating of it, man was to learn, by experience of the consequent punishment, the difference between the good of obedience and the evil of rebellion [5, page TODO]. St. Augustine emphasizes that the tree "was named not from the apples or fruit that grew on it but from what had followed after it had been touched against God's command" [4, page 42].

Still, why that specific name? Yes, the tree's name makes sense, but there are other names that also would have made sense. Why is it called the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil rather than, say, the Tree of Obedience or the Forbidden Tree?

St. John Chrysostom addresses this question by pointing out that it is the way with Sacred Scripture to name places from the things that happen, wherever it is they happen [3, TODO]. For example, another tree is named later in the book of Genesis: Deborah, Rebekah’s nurse, died. She was buried under the oak below Bethel, and so it was named Allon-bacuth (Genesis 35:8). The name "Allon-bacuth" can be translated "Oak of Weeping." Note that the Oak of Weeping surely does not have cursed acorns that magically cause people to weep. St. Pope John Paul II sheds more light on the Tree of Knowledge's name. He writes, By forbidding man to 'eat of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil', God makes it clear that man does not originally possess such 'knowledge' as something properly his own, but only participates in it by the light of natural reason and of Divine Revelation, which manifest to him the requirements and the promptings of eternal wisdom [6].

The knowledge itself is not forbidden

I would like to emphasize that God never forbids Adam and Eve from having knowledge of good and evil. I would argue that trying to better understand the nature of good and evil is praiseworthy. For example, when Solomon asks for a listening heart to judge your people and to distinguish between good and evil (1 Kings 3:9), God is pleased by Solomon's request (1 Kings 3:10). When Adam and Eve eat the forbidden fruit, they plunge humanity into an era of sin, pain, and death, but the silver lining is that at least they learn a lesson. The Tree of Knowledge is named for that silver lining.
